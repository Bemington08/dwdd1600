Digital Citizenship

What is Digital Citizenship?

Digital citizenship is the responsible, ethical, and safe use of technology to engage in online communities, encompassing behaviors like respecting others, protecting personal information, understanding digital laws, and promoting positive engagement, much like being a good citizen in the physical world.

What is Digital Law?

Digital law (or cyber law) is the body of law regulating activities and relationships in the digital world, covering legal rights and restrictions for using technology and the internet, addressing issues like data privacy, intellectual property, cybersecurity, online speech, and e-commerce. It establishes rules for responsible online behavior, holding users accountable for digital actions like hacking, copyright infringement, or identity theft, similar to real-world laws, with consequences for violations.

What is AUP?

AUP most commonly stands for Acceptable Use Policy, a set of rules defining how individuals can use an organization's technology (networks, computers, internet, etc.), outlining permitted and prohibited activities to ensure security, compliance, and responsible usage.

What is Plagiarism?

Plagiarism is presenting someone else's words, ideas, or work as your own without giving proper credit, essentially committing intellectual theft, whether intentionally or accidentally, and it involves failing to cite the original source.

It covers using text, concepts, artwork, or digital content from published or unpublished sources, even self-plagiarizing your own previously submitted work. Proper citation and attribution are key to avoiding it, as plagiarism carries serious academic and professional consequences.

What is Academic Dishonesty?

Academic dishonesty is any deceptive behavior in educational settings that provides an unfair advantage, including plagiarism, cheating, fabrication, and unauthorized collaboration, essentially claiming work or credit that isn't your own.
